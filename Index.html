Chapter 01

Correlation

Correlation measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables. It answers: "Do X and Y move together?"

The Big Picture — What is Correlation?

You have two variables, X and Y
e.g. Hours studied vs exam marks. You want to know if they move together.
You compute r — a single number between −1 and +1
+1 = perfect positive (both go up together). −1 = perfect negative (one up, other down). 0 = no linear relationship.
The key idea: deviation from the mean
If X is above its mean AND Y is above its mean → positive co-movement. The formula captures this pattern across all data points.

Pearson's r — Formula & Story

r = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) ÷ √[Σ(x−x̄)² × Σ(y−ȳ)²] Numerator = raw co-movement. Denominator = maximum possible co-movement. r = fraction of maximum achieved.
Numerator — Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ)

Product of deviations. Sign shows direction (+/−). Size shows co-movement strength. Points far from both means contribute more.

Denominator — √[Σ(x−x̄)² × Σ(y−ȳ)²]

Maximum possible covariance. We multiply the two spreads (like area). Square root removes squared units. This is the "ceiling".

Why divide? The normalisation story

r = actual co-movement ÷ ceiling. Always −1 to +1 regardless of data scale. Makes datasets comparable.

🔑
The n cancellation: Covariance = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ)/n and σ = √(Σ deviations²/n). When you compute r = Cov/(σx×σy), the n cancels completely. So the raw formula Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) / √[Σ(x−x̄)²·Σ(y−ȳ)²] already has n cancelled.

Spearman Rank Correlation

Why Spearman?

Pearson assumes linear relationships. Spearman converts raw data to ranks first — neutralising outliers and working with non-linear relationships. One outlier (say 500 hours) becomes rank 4 — same as any other rank 4 value.

ρ = 1 − 6Σd² ÷ n(n²−1) d = difference between rank of X and rank of Y for each pair. Σd²=0 → ρ=1 (perfect). Maximum Σd² → ρ=−1.
⚠️
Tied Ranks Rule: When two values tie, average the ranks they would have occupied. Two 5s taking ranks 4,5 → both get (4+5)/2 = 4.5. Next rank = 6. Three values tying ranks 1,2,3 → all get 2. Next = 4.
🚫
Common Trap: Always use the SAME ranking convention (1=highest or 1=lowest) for BOTH X and Y. Mixing conventions gives wrong d values.
Chapter 02

Regression

Regression builds on correlation to make predictions. While correlation asks "do they move together?", regression asks "given X, what is Y?" — and builds an equation to answer that.

Why Two Regression Lines?

Correlation describes the relationship. Regression predicts one variable from the other. The direction of prediction determines which line to use.

Y on X line

Use when: X is known, predict Y.
Minimises vertical errors (gaps in Y direction).
Formula: Y − ȳ = byx(X − x̄)

X on Y line

Use when: Y is known, predict X.
Minimises horizontal errors (gaps in X direction).
Formula: X − x̄ = bxy(Y − ȳ)

⚠️
The Shoe Analogy: You CANNOT rearrange Y on X to predict X. Rearranging algebraically flips the equation but does NOT change which errors are minimised. The line is still minimising vertical errors. It's like putting a left shoe on the right foot — it's still a left shoe.

Regression Coefficients

byx = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) ÷ Σ(x−x̄)² = r × (σy/σx) Slope of Y on X line. Same numerator as r. Denominator = spread of X only. Covariance ÷ Variance(X).
bxy = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) ÷ Σ(y−ȳ)² = r × (σx/σy) Slope of X on Y line. Same numerator. Denominator = spread of Y only. Covariance ÷ Variance(Y).
Key Relationships
  • byx × bxy = r²
  • r = √(byx × bxy)
  • Both coefficients have same sign as r
  • When r = ±1 → both lines are identical
  • When r = 0 → lines are perpendicular
  • Both lines pass through (x̄, ȳ)
Story of byx = Cov ÷ Var(X)

Covariance = how much X and Y move together. Variance(X) = how much X moves on its own. The ratio answers: "Of everything X does, how much does Y actually follow?" That ratio IS the slope.

WORKED EXAMPLE Both Regression Lines — Study Hours Data

Chapter 03

Hypothesis Testing — Core Theory

You observe something in a sample. Hypothesis testing asks: "Is this real, or is it just random chance?" Everything flows from this one question.

The Complete Story in One Flow

The Problem
You can never measure the entire population. You take a sample. Your sample mean will ALWAYS differ from the population mean — even if the population claim is 100% true. That's sampling randomness.
The Question
"Is my sample's deviation from the claim within normal randomness — or is it so extreme that it can no longer be explained by luck?"
The Answer
Set up two competing hypotheses. Compute a test statistic. Compare to a critical threshold (determined by your tolerance for error α). Make the decision.

Null & Alternative Hypotheses

H₀ — Null Hypothesis

The conservative, "nothing happened" claim. The default belief. Like innocent until proven guilty. Always contains = sign.

Example: H₀: μ = 70

H₁ — Alternative Hypothesis

Your challenger claim. What you're trying to prove. You never prove H₁ directly — you disprove H₀, and H₁ wins by default.

Example: H₁: μ ≠ 70 or μ > 70 or μ < 70

Level of Significance (α)

Definition

α is the probability of rejecting a TRUE null hypothesis (Type I error). It defines the boundary between "normal randomness" and "suspiciously extreme." α = 0.05 means: I will only reject H₀ if my result would happen by chance less than 5% of the time.

α = 0.05 (5%)

Most common. Accept a 5% risk of wrongly rejecting H₀. Critical Z = ±1.96 (two-tailed).

α = 0.01 (1%)

Stricter. Used for high-stakes decisions. Critical Z = ±2.576 (two-tailed).

α not given?

Default to α = 0.05. State your assumption clearly: "Since no level of significance is specified, we assume α = 0.05."

Type I and Type II Errors

H₀ is TRUE (innocent)
H₀ is FALSE (guilty)
Reject H₀
(Convict)
TYPE I ERROR
Convicting an innocent person. Probability = α. You control this.
Correct Decision
Guilty person convicted. Probability = 1−β (Power)
Accept H₀
(Acquit)
Correct Decision
Innocent person freed. Probability = 1−α
TYPE II ERROR
Freeing a guilty person. Probability = β. Harder to control.
🔑
The Cruel Tradeoff: You cannot minimise both errors simultaneously. Reducing α (stricter test) → fewer Type I errors BUT more Type II errors. The only real fix: increase sample size, which reduces both errors simultaneously.

One-Tailed vs Two-Tailed Tests

Two-Tailed (H₁: μ ≠ value)

No direction specified. Check both extremes. α splits equally: 2.5% each side. Critical Z = ±1.96 at 5%.

Keywords: "different from", "has changed", "is not equal to"

Left-Tailed (H₁: μ < value)

Suspect it's LOWER. All 5% on left side. Critical Z = −1.645 at 5%.

Keywords: "less than", "lower", "decreased", "at most"

Right-Tailed (H₁: μ > value)

Suspect it's HIGHER. All 5% on right side. Critical Z = +1.645 at 5%.

Keywords: "more than", "higher", "increased", "effective", "at least"

The 6-Step Procedure

1
State H₀ and H₁ — commit before seeing data
2
Set α — 0.05 or 0.01 (your tolerance for Type I error)
3
Choose the test — Z, t, chi-square, F (depends on data type and n)
4
Find critical value — from Z/t/chi-square/F table using α and df
5
Compute test statistic — plug numbers into formula
6
Compare and decide — is your statistic in the rejection zone?
Chapter 04

Z-Test — Large Sample Tests

Used when σ (population standard deviation) is KNOWN and n ≥ 30. The cleanest test. Everything else builds on this foundation.

Z-Test for Means

Z = (x̄ − μ₀) ÷ (σ/√n) SE = σ/√n = Standard Error. Bigger n → smaller SE → more sensitive test. Z tells you: how many SEs is your sample from the claimed mean?
🔑
The SE Story: σ measures how individual values vary. SE = σ/√n measures how sample MEANS vary. With 400 students, sample means cluster tightly — SE = 0.4. Even a 1-mark gap becomes suspicious. With only 5 students, SE is large — you need bigger gaps to be convincing.
WORKED EXAMPLE School Marks — Z-Test for Means

School claims μ=70. Sample: n=50, x̄=67, σ=15. Test at 5%.

1
H₀: μ=70    H₁: μ≠70   (no direction → two-tailed)
2
α=0.05, two-tailed → Critical Z = ±1.96
3
SE = 15/√50 = 15/7.071 = 2.121
4
Z = (67−70)/2.121 = −3/2.121 = −1.414
5
|Z calculated| = 1.414 < Z critical = 1.96 → Fail to reject H₀
Conclusion
School's claim is not disproved at 5%

Z-Test for Proportions

Z = (p̂ − p₀) ÷ √(p₀q₀/n) p̂ = sample proportion. p₀ = claimed proportion. q₀ = 1−p₀. Use when data is about proportions, not means.
🚫
Critical Values to Memorise:
α=0.05 two-tailed → ±1.96  |  α=0.01 two-tailed → ±2.576
α=0.05 one-tailed → ±1.645  |  α=0.01 one-tailed → ±2.326
Chapter 05

t-Test — Small Sample Tests

Used when σ is UNKNOWN and n < 30. You estimate σ using sample SD (s). The t-distribution is wider than Z to account for this extra uncertainty.

Why t and not Z?

Z-test condition

σ is KNOWN (given in problem). n ≥ 30.
Use Z table. No df needed.

t-test condition

σ is UNKNOWN. Only s (sample SD) given. n < 30.
Use t table with df = n−1.

🔑
The t distribution: Fatter tails than Z because using s introduces extra uncertainty. As df → ∞, t becomes Z. Larger df → t critical values get smaller → closer to Z values.

One Sample t-Test

t = (x̄ − μ₀) ÷ (s/√n)     df = n−1 CRITICAL: SE = s/√n. NOT s/√(n−1). The n−1 appears only INSIDE the formula for s itself, never in SE.
🚫
Most Common Exam Trap: SE = s/√n (correct) NOT s/√(n−1) (wrong). The n−1 lives only inside s = √[Σ(x−x̄)²/(n−1)]. Once s is computed, SE uses full n.

Two Sample t-Test

t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) ÷ [Sp × √(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)] df = n₁+n₂−2. Sp = pooled SD = √[((n₁−1)s₁² + (n₂−1)s₂²) ÷ (n₁+n₂−2)]

Paired t-Test

t = d̄ ÷ (Sd/√n)     df = n−1 d = After−Before for each pair. d̄ = mean of differences. Sd = SD of differences. H₀: μd = 0.
Which t-test to use?
SituationTestH₀df
One group vs claimed valueOne sample tμ = valuen−1
Two independent groupsTwo sample tμ₁ = μ₂n₁+n₂−2
Same people, before vs afterPaired tμd = 0n pairs − 1

Rejection Conditions — All Three Tests

Two-Tailed

Reject H₀ if |t calc| > t critical

Drop the sign. Use absolute value. Size matters, not direction.

Left-Tailed

Reject H₀ if t calc < −t critical

Sign matters directly. Must be sufficiently negative.

Right-Tailed

Reject H₀ if t calc > +t critical

Sign matters directly. Must be sufficiently positive.

Chapter 06

One-Way ANOVA

Analysis of Variance. Tests whether 3 or more group means are equal simultaneously — in one test, avoiding the accumulated error of multiple t-tests.

Why ANOVA? The Multiple Testing Problem

Wrong approach: Multiple t-tests

3 groups → 3 t-tests. Each has 5% error chance. Total error ≈ 14%. Not controlled. Unreliable.

Correct: ANOVA

Tests ALL groups simultaneously. Error stays exactly at 5%. One F-statistic. One decision.

The Core Idea — F Ratio

F = MSC ÷ MSE = Between-group variance ÷ Within-group variance F ≈ 1 → groups are similar (fail to reject H₀). F >> 1 → groups are different (reject H₀). Always right-tailed.

Professor's Shortcut Formulas (Exam Method)

The Formula Chain — Do These in Order
1
T = grand total of all values, N = total count
2
CF = T²/N (Correction Factor — used in every formula)
3
SSC = Σ(column total)²/n − CF (Between groups)
4
SST = Σ(every value)² − CF (Total)
5
SSE = SST − SSC (Within groups — never compute directly)
6
Fill ANOVA table → compute F = MSC/MSE
7
Compare F calc vs F critical (df1=c−1, df2=c(r−1) or N−c)

ANOVA Table Structure

SourceSSdfMS = SS÷dfF
Between (SSC)SSCc−1MSC = SSC/(c−1)MSC÷MSE
Within (SSE)SSEc(r−1)MSE = SSE/c(r−1)

✅
Verification check: SSC + SSE = SST always. If not, you made an arithmetic error. Also check: df between + df within = N−1.

Seeds A(20,21,23,16,20), B(18,20,17,15,25), C(25,28,22,28,32). c=3, r=5, N=15.

Seeds show significant yield differences
Chapter 07

Chi-Square Test

Works with categorical data and frequencies — not means. Compares what you OBSERVED vs what you EXPECTED. Same formula for both types; only how you compute E differs.

χ² = Σ (O − E)² ÷ E Sum over ALL categories/cells. Large χ² = big gap between observed and expected = reject H₀. Always right-tailed.

Type 1 — Goodness of Fit

Question answered

Does your data FIT a claimed distribution? One variable, one row of counts.

Finding E: E = Total observations × claimed proportion for each category.

df = k−1 where k = number of categories.

✅
Always verify: ΣO = ΣE (both equal total n). Σ(O−E) = 0 (deviations cancel). These are built-in error checks — if they fail, recheck your E values.

Type 2 — Independence of Attributes

Question answered

Are two variables related or independent? Two variables, a rows×columns contingency table.

Finding E: E = (Row total × Column total) ÷ Grand total for each cell.

df = (rows−1) × (columns−1)

🔑
2×2 table pattern: All four (O−E) values always have the same absolute value. If yours don't, recheck your E calculations.

Exam Questions — 5 & 10 Markers

5-Mark Questions

5 MARKS

Define correlation. What does r = +0.9 mean?

Define Karl Pearson's coefficient of correlation and interpret r = +0.9 and r = −0.3.

Definition: Karl Pearson's coefficient of correlation (r) is a statistical measure that quantifies the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables X and Y. It always lies between −1 and +1.

Formula: r = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) ÷ √[Σ(x−x̄)² × Σ(y−ȳ)²]

Interpretation:

  • r = +0.9: Strong positive correlation. As X increases, Y also increases. The relationship is nearly linear and very strong. 90% of the maximum possible co-movement is achieved.
  • r = −0.3: Weak negative correlation. As X increases, Y tends to decrease, but the relationship is weak and inconsistent. Much random scatter exists.
  • r = 0: No linear relationship.
  • r = ±1: Perfect linear relationship. All points fall exactly on a straight line.
5 MARKS

Explain Type I and Type II errors with examples.

What are Type I and Type II errors in hypothesis testing? How are they related to α and β?

Type I Error (α — False Positive): Rejecting H₀ when it is actually TRUE. Example: Declaring a medicine effective when it actually has no effect. Probability = α (the significance level you set). You directly control this by choosing α.

Type II Error (β — False Negative): Failing to reject H₀ when it is actually FALSE. Example: Declaring a medicine ineffective when it actually works. Probability = β. Harder to control — depends on sample size, effect size, and α.

The tradeoff: Reducing α (being stricter) reduces Type I errors but increases Type II errors. The only way to reduce both simultaneously is to increase the sample size.

Power = 1 − β = probability of correctly rejecting a false H₀. Higher power is desirable.

5 MARKS

Explain level of significance with an example.

What is the level of significance? Why is α = 0.05 commonly used?

Definition: The level of significance (α) is the probability of committing a Type I error — rejecting a null hypothesis that is actually true. It represents the maximum tolerable probability of making a wrong rejection decision.

Example: A school claims μ = 70 marks. You sample 50 students and get x̄ = 67. At α = 0.05, you are saying: "I will only reject the school's claim if this result would happen by pure chance less than 5% of the time when μ truly = 70."

α = 0.05 sets the acceptance range as 65.84 to 74.16 (70 ± 1.96 × SE). Since 67 falls inside this range, you fail to reject the claim.

Why 0.05? It is a balance — strict enough to avoid false alarms, lenient enough to still detect real effects. 0.01 is used when consequences of wrong rejection are severe (drug trials, medical decisions).

5 MARKS

Explain the two regression lines and why there are two.

Why are there two regression lines? When do you use each?

There are two regression lines because prediction is directional. Depending on which variable is known, a different line is used:

Y on X: Use when X is known and you want to predict Y. This line minimises the sum of squared VERTICAL distances (errors in Y direction). Equation: Y − ȳ = byx(X − x̄)

X on Y: Use when Y is known and you want to predict X. This line minimises the sum of squared HORIZONTAL distances (errors in X direction). Equation: X − x̄ = bxy(Y − ȳ)

Why can't you rearrange? Each line is built to minimise errors in a specific direction. Rearranging Y on X gives an equation that looks like X on Y algebraically, but still minimises vertical (not horizontal) errors. Using the wrong line gives incorrect predictions.

Both lines pass through: (x̄, ȳ) — the point of means — always.

10-Mark Questions

10 MARKS

Explain ANOVA with example and ANOVA table.

What is one-way ANOVA? Explain the procedure with a worked example.

What is ANOVA? Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a statistical technique used to test whether three or more group means are significantly different from each other — simultaneously, in one test.

Why not multiple t-tests? If you test 3 groups with 3 t-tests, each at 5% error, the total accumulated error ≈ 14%. ANOVA keeps it exactly at 5%.

Core idea — F ratio: ANOVA compares two sources of variation:

  • Between-group variance (MSC): How far apart are the group means from the overall mean? If groups truly differ, this will be large.
  • Within-group variance (MSE): How much do values vary inside each group? This is random noise — always present.

F = MSC ÷ MSE. If F ≈ 1, groups are similar. If F >> 1, groups differ significantly.

Hypotheses: H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃ (all equal). H₁: At least one group mean differs.

Worked Example: Three training programs, 4 employees each. Scores:

  • Program 1: 70, 72, 69, 71 (Total=282)
  • Program 2: 65, 68, 66, 67 (Total=266)
  • Program 3: 75, 78, 74, 77 (Total=304)

T=852, N=12, CF=852²/12=60492. SSC=182. SST=202. SSE=20.

10 MARKS

Chi-square — explain both types with examples.

Explain chi-square goodness of fit and independence test with worked examples.

What is chi-square? A test for categorical data (frequencies/counts) that compares observed frequencies against expected frequencies. Formula: χ² = Σ(O−E)²/E. Always right-tailed.

Type 1 — Goodness of Fit: Tests if data fits a claimed distribution. One variable. df = k−1.

Example: A die rolled 120 times gives: 14,18,20,22,24,22. If fair, E=20 each. χ² = (−6)²/20 + (−2)²/20 + 0 + (2)²/20 + (4)²/20 + (2)²/20 = 1.8+0.2+0+0.2+0.8+0.2 = 3.20. χ² critical (df=5) = 11.07. 3.20 < 11.07 → Die is fair.

Type 2 — Independence: Tests if two variables are related. Contingency table. df = (r−1)(c−1). E = (Row total × Col total) ÷ Grand total for each cell.

Example: 200 customers surveyed on gender vs brand preference. χ² = 2.02. Critical = 3.841. 2.02 < 3.841 → Gender and brand preference are independent.

Key difference: Same formula. Different method for computing E. Different df formula.

10 MARKS

Complete hypothesis testing procedure with Z-test example.

Explain the procedure for testing a hypothesis. A sample of 400 items from a normal population with mean 100 and SD 8 has sample mean 99. Test at 5%.

Procedure (6 steps):

Step 1 — State Hypotheses: H₀: μ = 100 (sample could come from this population). H₁: μ ≠ 100 (it could not). Two-tailed since no direction is specified.

Step 2 — Level of Significance: α = 0.05. Two-tailed → α/2 = 0.025 each side.

Step 3 — Critical Value: From Z table, area = 0.9750 → Z critical = ±1.96.

Step 4 — Compute Test Statistic:
SE = σ/√n = 8/√400 = 8/20 = 0.4
Z = (x̄ − μ₀)/SE = (99 − 100)/0.4 = −1/0.4 = −2.5

Step 5 — Decision: |Z calculated| = 2.5 > Z critical = 1.96 → Reject H₀.

Step 6 — Conclusion: At 5% level of significance, the sample does NOT appear to come from a population with mean 100. The deviation is statistically significant.

Why does 1 mark (99 vs 100) lead to rejection? With n=400, SE = 0.4 is tiny. Sample means cluster tightly around the true mean. Getting x̄=99 (2.5 SEs away) is extremely unlikely if μ truly = 100.

t-Test — complete procedure with example.

A soap company had mean sales of 140 dozens before an ad campaign. After the campaign, 26 shops showed mean 147 dozens with SD 16. Test if the advertisement is effective at 5%.

Why t-test? σ is unknown (only s=16 is given). n=26 < 30. One sample t-test.

Why right-tailed? "Effective" means sales INCREASED. Direction is upward. H₁: μ > 140.

Step 1: H₀: μ = 140. H₁: μ > 140. (Right-tailed)

Step 2: α = 0.05, right-tailed, df = n−1 = 25.

Step 3: From t-table, t critical (df=25, 5%, one-tailed) = 1.708.

Step 4: SE = s/√n = 16/√26 = 16/5.099 = 3.138.
t = (x̄ − μ₀)/SE = (147 − 140)/3.138 = 7/3.138 = 2.231

Step 5: t calculated = 2.231 > t critical = 1.708 → Reject H₀.

Conclusion: At 5% level of significance, the advertisement is effective. The increase from 140 to 147 dozens is statistically significant and cannot be attributed to random chance.

Spearman Rank Correlation — method and example.

When to prefer Spearman: When data is non-linear, when outliers are present, or when data is already in rank form. Spearman converts all values to ranks first — making it outlier-resistant. An extreme value like 500 just becomes rank 1.

Common Traps — Never Fall For These

Trap 1: Variance vs SD

If problem gives variance = 4, then σ = √4 = 2. Always square root variance before using in SE formula. Variance ≠ Standard Deviation.

Trap 2: SE formula

SE = s/√n — NOT s/√(n−1). The n−1 lives only inside the s formula itself. Once s is computed, SE uses full n always.

Trap 3: Sign of t

For two-tailed test: compare |t calculated| vs t critical. Drop the sign. For one-tailed: sign matters directly. −4.2 > −2.262 is FALSE. −4.2 < −2.262 is TRUE → reject.

Trap 4: Two regression lines

Never rearrange Y on X to predict X. Use the correct X on Y line. The shoe analogy: rearranging doesn't change which errors are minimised.

Trap 5: One vs two tail

"Effective", "increased", "at least" → right tail. "Decreased", "reduced", "at most" → left tail. "Different", "changed", "test the claim" → two tail. Read carefully.

Trap 6: Chi-square E values

Always verify: ΣO = ΣE and Σ(O−E) = 0. If either fails, your E values are wrong. Chi-square is always right-tailed — never one-tailed left.
